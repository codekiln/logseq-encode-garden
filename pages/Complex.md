- # Complex
	- ## Overview
		- A **complex** situation contains **many interacting parts** whose relationships produce **emergent** behavior: outcomes arise from **how the whole behaves**, not only from summing the parts.
		- Cause-and-effect is often **opaque beforehand** and becomes clearer **in retrospect** (“hindsight sensemaking”); useful progress usually blends **direction** with **experimentation** and **feedback**, not a single fixed blueprint.
	- ## Context
		- The term appears in everyday speech (“this is complex”) and in systems thinking (**complex adaptive systems**), organization theory, and frameworks such as the **Cynefin** framework, where **complex** marks the domain of **novel emergence** and **probe–sense–respond** leadership patterns—contrasted with **[[Compli/cat/ed]]** domains where **analysis** and **expertise** can yield a knowable best approach.
		- **Complex** does not simply mean “large” or “messy”; a huge but tightly constrained workflow can be **complicated** rather than **complex** in the technical sense.
	- ## Key principles
		- **Non-linear coupling** — Small changes can have **disproportionate** effects; effects can **feed back** through multiple pathways.
		- **Path dependence** — History matters; similar-looking starts can **diverge** because prior interactions shape what is possible next.
		- **Emergence** — Macro patterns (norms, accidents, performance cliffs, cultural dynamics) **arise** from micro interactions and constraints.
		- **Epistemic humility** — Multiple plausible explanations can coexist until you **intervene** and **observe** what actually happens in this context.
	- ## Compared to [[Compli/cat/ed]]
		- **Complex** — Expect **surprise in kind**, not only in quantity of detail; interventions are often **hypotheses** to be tested and revised.
		- **[[Compli/cat/ed]]** — Expect **difficulty in degree**: intricate, but often **analyzable** into components with **stable enough** rules that expertise can **predict** outcomes and recommend a strong approach.
		- **Overlap in practice** — Real systems mix both qualities (complicated interfaces sitting atop complex socio-technical dynamics); labeling helps choose **methods** (analysis vs iterative probing), not to pretend the world is only one or the other.
	- ## Examples
		- **Complex** — Organizational change where incentives, trust, and tooling interact; traffic and urban dynamics; ecosystems and markets with adaptive actors.
		- **Misleading usage** — Using “complex” as a synonym for “**I don’t want to explain it**” blurs the distinction; prefer naming **what** is uncertain (unknown constraints, political coupling, feedback loops).
