- # Enshittification
	- ## About
		- Nonfiction book by [[Person/Cory Doctorow]].
		- Examines why many digital platforms degrade over time and become hostile to users, creators, and smaller businesses.
		- Frames platform decline as a predictable outcome of concentrated market power, weak interoperability, and regulatory capture.
	- ## Conceptual Overview
		- [[Enshittification]] describes a recurring platform lifecycle:
			- 1. Platforms begin by over-delivering value to users to gain adoption.
			- 2. Once users are locked in, platforms shift value extraction toward business customers (advertisers, sellers, publishers, developers).
			- 3. After both sides are dependent, platforms extract maximum value for themselves, degrading outcomes for everyone else.
		- Core mechanism:
			- Control over discovery, ranking, and access lets platform owners unilaterally change rules.
			- Switching costs and network effects reduce exit options for users and suppliers.
			- Anti-competitive behavior is reinforced when interoperability is blocked.
		- Why it persists:
			- Short-term incentives favor rent extraction over long-term trust.
			- Market concentration reduces competitive pressure.
			- Policy and enforcement often lag behind technical and business changes.
		- What counters it:
			- Interoperability mandates and anti-lock-in design.
			- Strong competition policy and enforcement.
			- User rights around portability, transparency, and fair access.
