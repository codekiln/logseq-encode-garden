	- ## Notes
		- Public skepticism toward AI is rising, and this episode breaks the “anti-AI movement” into distinct concern groups instead of treating it as one monolith.
		- The episode argues that many critics are not anti-technology ideologues, but are reacting to concrete concerns (jobs, data centers, artist rights, child development, AI safety, and distrust of institutions).
		- The host frames this as politically meaningful: skepticism is broad enough that it could become a larger electoral/policy force by 2028.
	- ## Beehiiv episode takeaways (expanded)
		- Official episode page labels this as “Episode Links & Takeaways” on [[2026/02/24]] and includes a “Listen to the episode” link.
		- The anti-AI response is framed as real but heterogeneous: not one unified bloc, and better understood as a mix of motivations.
		- U.S. skepticism signals highlighted in the post:
			- YouGov: distrust and job-loss concern are both described as materially high.
			- Pew framing: U.S. appears unusually concern-heavy versus excitement-heavy in cross-country sentiment.
			- New Brunswick, NJ data center cancellation is used as a concrete example of local resistance becoming nationally visible.
		- Additional categories in the “mosaic of AI resistance” (beyond AI safety):
			- Capability skeptics: believe current systems are overhyped or plateauing; timeline skeptics expect slower workplace diffusion.
			- AI bubblers: may accept technological progress but question valuations, business models, and current market pricing.
			- Artist advocates: center fairness, copyright/IP, and perceived extraction from creative communities.
			- “Slop” secessionists: cultural rejection of AI outputs, often as a marker of broader anti-AI sentiment.
			- Child safety advocates: concerns about youth dependency, social development, and relationship effects.
			- Data center deniers/opponents: local-grid, water, land-rights, and community-cost concerns; episode argues these are often poorly addressed by industry.
			- Job displacement coalition: presented as the largest and politically broadest concern cluster.
			- Big Tech haters: anti-concentration sentiment plus long-tail social-media disillusionment shaping AI reception.
		- The post’s closing argument is that discourse is still pliable: many critics are issue-specific rather than absolutist, so concrete policy/process improvements could shift some resistance into cautious optimism.
