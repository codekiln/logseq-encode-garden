- # Heuristic vs Axiom
	- ## Overview
		- A **heuristic** is a useful shortcut: a rule of thumb that usually points the right way but carries no guarantee and can fail when conditions change. An **axiom** is a statement that can be proven with internal consistency, or an established law of nature—it holds by demonstration, not by convenience.
		- The distinction matters because the two are easily confused. A heuristic that held reliably in one era gets repeated until it is treated as a law, long after the conditions that made it true have passed.
	- ## Why it matters
		- Treating a heuristic as an axiom hides its assumptions. When those assumptions lapse, the "law" keeps getting cited with the same confidence, and the error compounds because no one re-examines a premise they believe is proven.
		- The garden tracks the two families separately: defeasible, named generalizations are [[Logseq/Entity/Named Law]] instances; genuinely provable or physical statements are [[Logseq/Entity/Axiom]] instances.
