- # What's the difference between a [[Metaphor]], a [[Simile]], and an [[Analogy]]? [[Card]]
	- ## [[AI Answer]]
		- **Short answer:** A [[Metaphor]] says one thing *is* another ("Time is a thief"). A [[Simile]] is a metaphor's explicit cousin — it compares using *like* or *as* ("Time is like a thief"). An [[Analogy]] is bigger than either: it's a structured comparison used to **explain or argue**, mapping several corresponding parts between two things, often built *out of* a metaphor or simile.
		- | Device | What it asserts | Marker | Primary purpose |
		  | -------- | -------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------- | ----------------------------- |
		  | **Metaphor** | One thing *is* another (implied identity) | none — the equation is implicit | Vividness, compression, poetry |
		  | **Simile** | One thing is *like* another | the words "like" or "as" | Vivid but explicit comparison |
		  | **Analogy** | Two things share a *structure* of relationships | often phrased "A is to B as C is to D" | Explanation or argument |
		- ### Metaphor vs. simile
			- The only surface difference is the comparison word. A simile keeps "like" or "as" and states the comparison openly; a metaphor drops them and asserts the identity directly, which reads as stronger and more compressed.
				- Simile: "Her mind is **like** a steel trap."
				- Metaphor: "Her mind **is** a steel trap."
			- Many grammarians treat a simile as a **subtype of metaphor** — both are *figures of speech* (rhetorical/poetic devices) and both work by likeness; the simile just signals the likeness with an explicit marker.
		- ### How an analogy differs from both
			- An analogy is less a figure of speech and more a **mode of reasoning or explanation**. Where a metaphor or simile makes a single vivid likeness, an analogy draws out **multiple, systematic correspondences** to clarify something unfamiliar or to support an argument.
			- It is usually **extended** and **functional** rather than ornamental: "A cell's nucleus is like the brain of the cell — it stores the instructions, issues commands, and the rest of the cell carries them out." The point isn't poetic color; it's to make biology graspable.
			- Classic analogies often take the proportional form **"A is to B as C is to D"** (e.g. "wheels are to a car as legs are to a person").
		- ### Rule of thumb
			- See "like" or "as" → **simile**. A direct *is/are* equation → **metaphor**. An extended comparison doing explanatory or argumentative work → **analogy** (which may itself contain metaphors and similes).
