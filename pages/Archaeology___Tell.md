- # [Tell (archaeology)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tell_(archaeology))
	- ## Overview
		- A **tell** (from Arabic *tall*, “mound, small hill”) is an **artificial mound**: stratified debris from **many successive settlements** built on the **same footprint**, plus natural sediment—not a natural hill.
		- Tells are the signature landform of **multi-period occupation** in archaeology: each layer encodes a phase of building, abandonment, and rebuilding, so the mound is both a **landscape feature** and a **vertical archive** of human use.
	- ## Context
		- Tells are most strongly associated with the **ancient Near East** (Upper Mesopotamia, Southern Levant, Anatolia, Iran) where **continuous settlement** in less arid zones allowed debris to outpace erosion.
		- The pattern also appears in **Southern and Central Europe** (e.g. Greece, Bulgaria, Hungary, Spain), **North Africa**, and **West Africa**—anywhere accumulation can win over truncation.
		- In the **Southern Levant**, the tell-dominated settlement pattern largely ends with the **Hellenistic** shift after **Alexander the Great**; elsewhere many tells remain **occupied today**.
		- English *tell* entered archaeological usage in the 1840s; field spellings include **tel**, **tall**, **til**, and **tal** depending on source language and transliteration convention.
	- ## Key Principles
		- **Stratified time depth** — Superimposed cultural layers (often labeled by period or phase) make tells prime targets for **stratigraphic excavation**.
		- **Net accumulation** — A tell forms only when **deposition exceeds removal** (erosion, deliberate leveling, modern earthmoving); that constraint explains the tell’s **geographic clustering**.
		- **Terminology is regional** — Equivalent mound words include **tepe** / **tappeh** (Turkish/Persian), **hüyük** / **höyük** (Turkish), **kom** (Egyptian Arabic), and **chogha** (Persian); **khirbet** (“ruin”) often labels ruined tells in toponyms but is not synonymous with *tell*.
		- **Toponymy** — *Tell* is among the most common prefixes in Palestinian place names; the same root appears in biblical Hebrew **tel** and in Akkadian/Sumerian cognates debated by philologists.
	- ## Mechanism
		- Mound material mixes **organic and cultural refuse**, **collapsed mudbrick** and other building rubble, **water-laid** and **aeolian** sediments, and residues of biogenic and geochemical processes on site.
		- The classic profile is a **low truncated cone** with **sloping sides** and a **flat, mesa-like summit**; large examples exceed **40 m** in height and can cover tens of hectares at the base.
		- A tell is **not born as a mound**: the earliest occupation is a **flat surface**; nucleation of households and repeated rebuilding of **daub** structures over generations raises the horizon—often over **centuries** before the form reads clearly as a tell.
	- ## Examples
		- **Near East** — [Tell es-Sultan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tell_es-Sultan) (Jericho) among the earliest known examples; [Tell Barri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tell_Barri) in Syria; the [Citadel of Aleppo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Citadel_of_Aleppo) atop a tell occupied since the third millennium BCE; [Tel Megiddo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tel_Megiddo) in Israel. Surveys have cataloged **thousands** of tells in the Southern Levant alone.
		- **Europe** — Neolithic and Chalcolithic tells in **northeastern Bulgaria**; Greek **toumbas** (Macedonia) and **magoulas** (Thessaly), with debate over **communal nucleation** (Chapman) versus **household-scale** accumulation (Halstead).
		- **Scale** — [Tell Barri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tell_Barri) reaches on the order of **30+ m** height with a large basal footprint, illustrating how long occupation can dominate local topography.
	- ## Misconceptions
		- “Every prominent hill in the Levant is a tell” — **False**; relief must be explained by **anthropogenic buildup** and **stratified settlement**, not geology alone.
		- “Tell and tel are interchangeable spellings for one language” — **Oversimplified**; *tel* often marks **Hebrew** toponym transliteration, *tell* **Arabic**—same concept, different conventions.
		- “A tell is always abandoned” — **False**; many tells are **still lived on**; *khirbet* (“ruin”) in a name signals destruction, not the tell concept itself.
		- “You can date a tell from its exterior shape alone” — **False**; chronology comes from **excavation**, survey, and **material culture** in strata, not from silhouette.
