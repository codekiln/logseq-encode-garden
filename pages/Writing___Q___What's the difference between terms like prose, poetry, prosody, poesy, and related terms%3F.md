- # What's the difference between terms like prose, poetry, prosody, poesy, and related terms?
	- ## [[AI Answer]]
		- **Short answer:** the real formal split is [[Prose]] vs. [[Verse]], not prose vs. poetry. [[Poetry]] is the broader art form that's usually written in verse but doesn't have to be. [[Prosody]] studies the sound patterns underneath verse and speech. [[Poesy]] is just an archaic word for poetry.
		- ### [[Prose]] vs. [[Verse]] — the real formal split
			- The most basic split in written language isn't "prose vs. poetry" — it's prose vs. verse. Prose runs to the margin and wraps naturally; verse is deliberately broken into lines, usually to a metrical or rhythmic pattern.
		- ### [[Poetry]] vs. [[Verse]]
			- Verse describes the *form*: metrical, lineated language. Poetry describes the *effect*: heightened, musical, imagistic language used to convey feeling or meaning. Most poetry is written in verse, but prose poetry shows the two can separate — it keeps poetry's devices (compression, imagery, rhythm, figurative language) while running as unbroken prose.
		- ### [[Prosody]]
			- Prosody doesn't name a genre or form at all — it names the sound system underneath verse (and, in linguistics, underneath speech generally): meter, stress, rhythm, pitch, intonation. Scanning a poem's meter is doing prosody.
		- ### [[Poesy]]
			- Poesy is an older word (Middle English, via French, from Greek *poiesis*, "making") that simply means poetry or the art of poetry. It survives mainly in older or self-consciously archaic writing (e.g., Sir Philip Sidney's *The Defence of Poesy*) and carries no distinct modern meaning beyond that — treat it as a period synonym for poetry.
