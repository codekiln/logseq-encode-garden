- # [Cogito and the History of Madness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cogito_and_the_History_of_Madness)
	- ## Summary
		- "Cogito and the History of Madness" is Jacques Derrida's 1963 paper responding to [[Person/Michel Foucault/Book/History of Madness]].
		- Derrida questions Foucault's reading of Descartes, especially whether the Cartesian "cogito" (I think) excludes madness or instead must pass through a more radical threat to reason.
		- The paper was presented in 1963, published in *Revue de metaphysique et de morale*, revised for *Writing and Difference*, and translated into English by Alan Bass in 1978.
		- The essay helped initiate a major exchange between Derrida and Foucault around madness, reason, and the conditions of philosophical discourse.
	- ## Notes
		- Derrida opens with two quotes that frame madness as a limit case for reason, choice, and writing.
		- [[Person/Søren Kierkegaard]]'s line, "The instant of decision is madness," refers to how a real decision appears only where reason no longer clearly identifies the superior option.
		- [[Person/James Joyce]]'s line about [[Book/Ulysses]] "In any event this book was terribly daring. A transparent sheet separates it from madness" places daring writing at a threshold near madness: a work of original writing is separated from madness only by a fragile boundary.
		- These prepare Derrida's examination of whether philosophy can mark madness as an "outside" without *depending on* that outside from *within* reason's own operation.
		- Derrida's dispute with Foucault centers on whether Descartes excludes madness too quickly or whether the evil demon hypothesis already admits a more radical disturbance of reason.
