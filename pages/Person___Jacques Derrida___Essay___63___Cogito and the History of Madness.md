- # [Cogito and the History of Madness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cogito_and_the_History_of_Madness)
	- ## Summary
		- "Cogito and the History of Madness" is Jacques Derrida's 1963 paper responding to Michel Foucault's *History of Madness*.
		- Derrida questions Foucault's reading of Descartes, especially whether the Cartesian cogito excludes madness or instead must pass through a more radical threat to reason.
		- The paper was presented in 1963, published in *Revue de metaphysique et de morale*, revised for *Writing and Difference*, and translated into English by Alan Bass in 1978.
		- The essay helped initiate a major exchange between Derrida and Foucault around madness, reason, and the conditions of philosophical discourse.
	- ## Notes
		- Derrida opens with two epigraphs that frame madness as a limit case for reason, choice, and writing.
		- [[Person/Søren Kierkegaard]]'s line, "The instant of decision is madness," matters because a real decision appears only where reason no longer clearly identifies the superior option.
		- [[Person/James Joyce]]'s line about [[Book/Ulysses]] places daring writing at a threshold near madness: the work is not simply irrational, but separated from madness by a fragile boundary.
		- Together, the epigraphs prepare Derrida's question about whether philosophy can mark madness as an outside without depending on that outside from within reason's own operation.
		- Derrida's dispute with Foucault centers on whether Descartes excludes madness too quickly or whether the evil demon hypothesis already admits a more radical disturbance of reason.
