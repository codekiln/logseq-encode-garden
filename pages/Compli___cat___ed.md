- # Complicated
	- ## Overview
		- Something **complicated** has **many parts, dependencies, or steps**, so it can feel difficult or expensive to work with—but the underlying situation is still **knowable and analyzable** in principle.
		- With enough **expertise**, time, and decomposition, you can often **trace causes**, **predict outcomes**, and apply a **right answer** or a **good procedure** (even if discovering it was painful).
	- ## Context
		- The word is casual English (“this deployment is *complicated*”) and also technical in **sensemaking** frameworks (for example the **Cynefin** framework), where **complicated** names the domain of **known unknowns** resolved by **analysis** and **good practice**, contrasted with **[[Complex]]** situations where cause and effect **emerge** and are clearer **only after the fact**.
		- Complicated work can still be **hard**; the distinction is not “easy vs hard” but **decomposability and predictability** of the system’s behavior under study.
	- ## Key principles
		- **Decomposition** — Subproblems can be **separated**, owned, and reasoned about with **expert judgment** and **established methods**.
		- **Legibility** — With enough mapping, the system’s structure and interaction rules can be made **explicit** (diagrams, contracts, runbooks).
		- **Optimizable** — There is often a meaningful sense of **better vs worse** designs once constraints and goals are stated.
	- ## Compared to [[Complex]]
		- **Complicated** — Relationships are **intricate** but often **stable enough** that experts can **forecast** what will happen when you change X; surprises tend to come from **scale**, **detail**, or **missing information**, not from **novel emergence** every time you intervene.
		- **[[Complex]]** — Many interacting forces produce **emergent** behavior; the same intervention can **land differently** depending on history and context; useful knowledge is often built through **probing**, **feedback**, and **safe-to-fail** experiments, not a single exhaustive blueprint.
		- **Practical heuristic** — If the hard part is **doing all the work carefully**, it may be **complicated**. If the hard part is that **the situation keeps teaching you something new** when you touch it, it may be **complex**.
	- ## Examples
		- **Complicated** — A large but **well-specified** migration with many moving parts; tuning a **known** performance model with many knobs; a dense legal agreement where experts can still say what clauses imply.
		- **Not automatically complex** — “Many components” alone does not imply **[[Complex]]**; a complicated assembly line can still be **mostly predictable** if interactions are constrained and monitored.
