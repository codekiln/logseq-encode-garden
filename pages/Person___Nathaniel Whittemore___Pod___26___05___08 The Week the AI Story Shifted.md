- # Nathaniel Whittemore, "The Week the AI Story Shifted," The AI Daily Brief, May 8 2026.
- {{video https://youtu.be/ef4WR5vUJro?t=1377}}
	- ## {{youtube-timestamp 1378}} Discussion of [[Codex Goal Mode]]
		- Whittemore focused less on the novelty of autonomous looping itself and more on what happens when this capability becomes reliable enough to integrate into ordinary workflows. He quoted developers who had agents running overnight on technical problems, sometimes for more than a dozen hours at a time. The implication was not merely convenience. It was a change in the shape of knowledge work.
		- One part of the discussion that stood out was his distinction between building a system and operating a system. He described using GPT-5.5 to reason through whether one of his own projects was appropriate for Slash Goal. The project involved turning podcast episodes into structured knowledge assets for teams. The model reportedly argued that the system architecture itself was a standard coding project, but the daily processing pipeline was a strong candidate for persistent goal execution. That distinction matters.
		- Slash Goal appears best suited for tasks that are persistent, inspectable, and verifiable. The agent needs a stable objective and some way to evaluate progress. A coding task with test suites fits naturally into that shape. A repeated content transformation pipeline also fits. A vague request like "make this better" does not.
		- Whittemore also emphasized something many people overlook when discussing autonomous agents. The bottleneck is often not the model. It is the framing of the work. He argued that weak prompts make Slash Goal behave like a normal chatbot session stretched over a longer period. The more effective approach was what he called meta prompting.
		- His suggested workflow was recursive. First, ask another model to research the `/goal` feature itself. Then ask it to examine the project you want to automate. Then ask it to generate several candidate persistent-goal prompts optimized for that project. Only after that do you hand the resulting specification to the long-running agent.
		- That approach treats prompting less like issuing commands and more like designing operational scaffolding. The human defines constraints, evaluation criteria, formats, and boundaries. The agent handles iteration inside that frame.
		- Whittemore connected this trend to a broader transition happening across the AI industry. He argued that the field is entering a "harness engineering" phase. Raw model intelligence already exceeds what many organizations can operationalize. The real work now involves memory systems, orchestration layers, review pipelines, persistent agents, and tooling that keeps models aligned with long-running objectives.
		- Seen through that lens, Slash Goal is not just another coding feature. It is part of a larger attempt to turn language models from reactive assistants into ongoing computational labor. The important question is no longer whether a model can generate a clever response. It is whether a system can sustain directed effort over time without losing coherence.
		- That changes the economics of AI usage. If agents can remain active for hours or days while incrementally advancing toward a goal, token consumption scales in a different direction. More importantly, the role of the human shifts. The valuable skill stops being rapid-fire prompting and becomes the design of environments where autonomous work can proceed safely and productively.
